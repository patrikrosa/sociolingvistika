01Components of Communication+

Communication is a process with six interconnected elements:

ParticipantsSender (source) and Receiver (destination)
ProcessEncoding — forming the message · Decoding — interpreting it
MessageInformation (the idea) + Language Code (the system used) + Signal (the physical output)
MediumChannel (the path) + Signal Transfer (the delivery)
Sender → Encode → [Info + Code + Signal] → Channel → Decode → Receiver
02Three Types of Information+
ConceptualThe abstract thought before it becomes language. Universal — not tied to any specific language.
LinguisticThe form a concept takes in a specific language. E.g. the concept of "tree" → tree in English, strom in Slovak.
MaterialThe physical properties of the signal — voice pitch, tone quality, channel characteristics.
03–05Saussure's Trichotomy: Langue, Parole, Langage+
LangageThe broadest term — the general human capacity for language. The umbrella over everything.
LangueThe shared abstract system — grammar, vocabulary, rules held collectively by a community. The "rulebook."
ParoleThe actual individual speech act — real, concrete language use in a specific moment.
Chess: Langage = the concept of chess. Langue = the rules. Parole = the game you played Tuesday.
06Chomsky: Competence vs. Performance+
CompetenceThe internalized, abstract knowledge of a language. The "perfect" grammar system in the speaker's head.
PerformanceActual language use in real life — imperfect, affected by fatigue, distraction, and memory lapses.
Exam trap: Very similar to Saussure's Langue/Parole. Key difference — Saussure focuses on the social system; Chomsky focuses on the individual mind. (Chomsky was in the Epstein files — sus.)
07Sociolinguistics+

The study of language in relation to society. Examines how social factors — class, age, location, occupation — influence how people speak.

Core question: "How does society change language?" Language is the starting point. Also called Micro-sociolinguistics.
08Linguosociology (Sociology of Language)+

Also called Macro-sociolinguistics. Studies society through language — how language shapes social structures, national identity, and policy.

Core question: "How does language change society?" Society is the starting point.

Example: how declaring one language the national standard affects the identity of ethnic minorities.

09Micro vs. Macro Sociolinguistics+

The distinction is your starting point:

Micro (Socioling.)Starts with Language. Look at a linguistic variation → trace it to a social group.
Macro (Linguosociol.)Starts with Society. Look at a social group → determine the linguistic features that define them.
Micro = Language → Society. Macro = Society → Language.
10Theoretical vs. Applied Sociolinguistics+
TheoreticalBuilds models and frameworks. Academic — creates general rules for how language works in social contexts.
AppliedUses those theories for real-world problems: language planning, curriculum design, national language policy.
11Variety (Lect)+

A neutral umbrella term for any specific form of language used by a particular group. Defined by a consistent set of linguistic items (sounds, words, grammar) sharing the same social, geographical, or situational distribution.

Preferred over "dialect" because it carries no political or prestige baggage.

12Language vs. Dialect+
LanguageHigh social prestige, backed by a state or institution. Different languages are typically not mutually intelligible.
DialectA variation within a language. Usually mutually intelligible (e.g. British vs. American English).
"A language is a dialect with an army and a navy." — Max Weinreich. Norwegian and Swedish are mutually intelligible but called separate languages due to national borders. Very sus.
13Dialect Levelling (Normalization)+

The natural, uncontrolled process by which unique regional features disappear as speakers converge toward a shared majority norm. Happens organically through contact and mobility — not by institutional decree.

vs. StandardizationStandardization is deliberate — imposed through schools and government. Dialect levelling is spontaneous.
14Types of Dialects+
SociolectSocial dialect — defined by social class, age, or occupation. (Teen slang, professional jargon, working-class accent.)
GeolectRegional dialect — defined by geographical location. (Yorkshire English, Slovak from Bratislava vs. Košice.)
15Overt vs. Covert Prestige+
Overt PrestigeSeeking status by using the standard variety — the language of education, law, media. Publicly acknowledged.
Covert PrestigeSeeking status by using a non-standard variety. Shows loyalty to a community or subculture. Hidden from mainstream view but real within the group.
Example: using local slang to signal authenticity to your friend group, even though you "know better."
16Vernacular+

The informal, everyday speech acquired at home as a first language. It is uncodified — not governed by official grammar rules. Represents the most natural and spontaneous form of communication in private settings.

InformalUncodifiedFirst languageHome speech
17Pidgins+

A simplified contact language that emerges when groups with no common language need to communicate — typically for trade. Limited grammar, limited vocabulary, draws from multiple source languages.

Critical: No native speakers. A pidgin is always a second language for everyone who uses it. This is the single most testable point.
18Superstrate vs. Substrate+

In a pidgin, the two source languages play different roles:

SuperstrateThe dominant language (colonizer, trading power). Contributes most of the vocabulary.
SubstrateThe subordinate language (local/enslaved population). Influences grammar and phonology.
Super = above = power = vocabulary. Sub = below = foundation = grammar structure.
19Creole+

A creole is a pidgin that has acquired native speakers. When children grow up in a pidgin-speaking community and adopt it as their first language, the language undergoes de-simplification — grammar complexifies, vocabulary expands.

Pidgin (no native speakers) → children acquire it as L1 → Creole (full native language)
20Speech Community+

A group of people who interact regularly and share two things: a common language or dialect, and a shared set of social norms for how and when to use it.

It is not enough to share a language — the group must share the rules of use: when formal register is appropriate, when slang is acceptable, etc.
21Community of Practice+

A group united by a shared enterprise — a job, a hobby, a sport — who develop shared ways of talking through mutual engagement. Language is a byproduct of doing things together.

vs. Speech CommunitySpeech community = defined by shared language norms. Community of Practice = defined by shared activity.
Among Us crew doing tasks together = community of practice. They develop "sus," "vent," "vote" — shared language from shared activity.
22Communicative Competence+

Coined by Dell Hymes. Expanded Chomsky's "competence" to include social knowledge — not just knowing the grammar but knowing how to use language appropriately.

Linguistic competenceGrammatical knowledge — what is structurally correct.
Sociolinguistic competenceSocial knowledge — what is appropriate: when, with whom, in what context.
23Units of Communication+

Three nested levels, largest to smallest:

Communicative SituationThe overall context — a lecture, a party, an emergency meeting.
Communicative EventA specific activity within that situation — a toast, a vote, an accusation.
Communicative ActA single speech act — "That's sus," "I saw you vent," "I vote red."
24Approaches to Linguistic Research+
Qualitative vs. QuantitativeMeaning and interpretation vs. numerical data and statistical analysis.
Synchronic vs. DiachronicLanguage at one point in time vs. language change over time.
Inductive vs. DeductiveData → theory (inductive) vs. theory → testing (deductive).
25What is a Variable?+

A variable is a linguistic or social category that contains a set of alternatives, each called a variant (or value).

Example: future tense is a variable. Its variants are "gonna" vs. "will" — same meaning, different social context.
26Types of Linguistic Variables (at least 3)+
PhoneticSound variation — e.g. dropping the /h/ in "house" → "'ouse"
MorphologicalWord-form variation — e.g. "dived" vs. "dove"
LexicalWord-choice variation — e.g. "lift" (UK) vs. "elevator" (US)
27Types of Social Variables (at least 5)+

Features of speakers that correlate with linguistic variation:

Social classAgeGenderRaceEthnicity
28Types of Situational Variables (at least 5)+

Features of the communicative situation that influence language use:

MediumFieldGenreStyleSetting
29Indicator, Marker, Stereotype+

Three levels of a linguistic variable, ordered by degree of speaker awareness:

IndicatorGroup variation exists. Speakers are unaware. Does not shift with speech style.
MarkerGroup variation exists. Speakers have some awareness. Changes between formal and informal speech.
StereotypeGroup variation exists. Speakers are fully conscious of it. Publicly discussed, mocked, or imitated.
Awareness scale: Indicator (none) → Marker (some) → Stereotype (full). Think: volume dial.
30What is a Hypothesis?+

A scientific assumption — a tentative answer to a research problem. It specifies a measurable relationship between variables that can be tested through systematic observation. It is not a fact; it is a testable prediction.

TestableMeasurableTentativeFalsifiable

Saussure vs. Chomsky — How they divide language

SaussureChomsky
Langue — shared abstract system of a community (social)Competence — perfect grammar in one individual's head (psychological)
Parole — individual speech acts in real lifePerformance — actual, imperfect speech (fatigue, distraction, etc.)
Key difference: Saussure = social system. Chomsky = individual mind. Both separate the "rulebook" from the "use."

Sociolinguistics (Micro) vs. Linguosociology (Macro)

Sociolinguistics — MicroLinguosociology — Macro
Starts with language featuresStarts with social groups
Asks: how does society shape language?Asks: how does language shape society?
Studies slang/accents → links to class/age/placeStudies ethnic groups → links to policy and identity
Same subject matter, opposite direction. It's about your starting point.

Pidgin vs. Creole

PidginCreole
No native speakers — always a second languageHas native speakers — acquired by children as L1
Simplified grammar and vocabularyDe-simplification — fuller grammar and vocab emerge
Created for specific need (trade, contact)Develops when a community adopts pidgin as its primary language
Creole is a nativized pidgin. Process: Pidgin → children adopt it as L1 → Creole.

Speech Community vs. Community of Practice

Speech CommunityCommunity of Practice
Defined by shared language + shared norms of useDefined by shared activity or enterprise
Can be large (a city, a country)Usually smaller and specific (a team, a crew, a department)
Members defined by how they speakLanguage is a byproduct of doing things together
Among Us players who know the lingo = speech community. The specific crew who plays together daily and develops private terms = community of practice.

Overt vs. Covert Prestige

Overt PrestigeCovert Prestige
Using the standard/formal variety for statusUsing a non-standard/local variety for status
Publicly acknowledged, mainstream recognitionHidden from mainstream; real only within the in-group
Language of education, law, broadcast mediaLocal accent, community slang, subculture dialect
Both are about social status. The difference is whose status, and which community recognizes it.
